# E-Drum Practice Tool — Capture Device Architecture & Decision Log

*Living spec. Companion to **edrum-brain-spec.md**. Sections marked **DECISION** are open; **⚠ VERIFY** marks claims that must be confirmed against a schematic/datasheet before you commit money or a build. Everything else is the agreed shape.*

---

## 0. Purpose & the seam

This is the *other* machine. Per the two-machine split (brain spec §1), the capture device is deliberately **dumb, realtime, deterministic**, and the brain is smart and deferred. They are joined by exactly one thing — the **session file** — and that contract lives in the brain spec (§3). This document owns everything on the device side of that seam and nothing on the other side.

**The device's entire output is:** raw `(t, note, velocity)` events + a reassignable `kit_profile_id` + the metadata the brain needs (grid segments on grade-span declaration, bookmarks, session boundaries). It performs **zero analysis** and **zero normalization** — those are brain-side and re-runnable (brain spec §4.2).

**Deferral note (read first):** the device is **not** on the critical path to the brain. In the laptop phase the "capture device" is just the kit → Reaper/`mido` on a computer, which emits the same events over a live port. The physical box is a **later, contained spike** whose only job is to eventually emit the same session files the brain already consumes. Build the brain first; build this when you want the no-PC standalone unit (or the firmware learning). Nothing here blocks the brain.

---

## 1. The three jobs (and the principle that assigns them)

The device does exactly three things:

1. **Host the kit** over USB and receive its MIDI.
2. **Timestamp** each event at the instant of arrival.
3. **Persist** raw `(t, note, velocity)` + metadata.

Plus one output it *produces* rather than receives: **the click** (it is the tempo authority).

**The assignment principle** — the reusable test for every "device or brain?" question: *is the information destroyed if I don't do it now?*
- **Timestamp → device.** The clock is gone the instant the byte lands; it cannot be reconstructed later. This is why timestamping *must* be on the device.
- **Normalization, grading → brain.** The raw note is preserved, so these can always be re-derived. They must *not* be on the device (baking them in at the one unfixable point).

The device commits only to what can't be reconstructed: the timestamp, and the raw note as received. Everything else is the brain's, revisably, forever.

## 2. Firmware architecture — the realtime path

The one hard constraint: **never drop a MIDI event, and never let storage I/O corrupt a timestamp.** Everything else is comfortable (drum MIDI is a low rate — a few hundred events/sec at peak).

**Ring buffer between capture and storage.** The capture path (ISR / arrival callback) writes events into a ring buffer; a separate consumer drains the buffer to SD. This isolates the capture edge from SD write-blocking, so a slow flush never stalls capture and never loses events. (This is the capstone manuscript's "ring buffer isolates the capture ISR from SD I/O blocking" — still correct, and *more* relevant on the MAX3421E path where polling must interleave with SD writes.)

**Two cores (S3).** Capture + click on one core, storage/housekeeping on the other, so a long SD write can't jitter the click or delay capture servicing.

## 3. Timestamp discipline — the load-bearing correctness item

Timestamp quality *is* the product's data quality (this is a timing tool). Two rules:

**Rule 1 — one clock.** The timestamp and the click must be read from the **same clock**, because grading compares hit-time against click-schedule. The click is generated by the device, so the timestamp must be taken by the device. (Corollary: you can't offload timestamping to an external chip that doesn't know the click clock.)

**Rule 2 — stamp at the arrival edge, not at your convenience.** Stamp the moment data is *available*, before any storage work, or your loop's jitter (dominated by SD writes) smears the timestamp.
- **Native USB host:** stamp in the arrival callback; the native stack background-buffers via ISR/DMA, so this is robust by default.
- **MAX3421E (external host):** stamp in the **INT-pin ISR** — grab `micros()` the instant the chip signals data-ready — then read the bytes over SPI afterward. Do this and MAX timestamps equal native's. Skip it and you eat poll/SD jitter. (Same ring-buffer-ISR pattern as §2.)

**The floor.** Both paths inherit USB full-speed's **~1 ms frame quantization** — the module is polled on a 1 ms heartbeat, so every hit is quantized to ~1 ms regardless of MCU. That is the hard floor, and it's fine: grading targets 5–30 ms deviations, and the constant part of any latency calibrates out (§4). No MCU gets below it; none needs to.

## 4. The click generator (device = tempo authority)

The device generates the click and **owns the grid**, because the app/device being the tempo authority is what makes the grid exact-by-construction rather than estimated (brain spec §1A).

**No built-in DAC.** The ESP32-S3 has **no analog DAC** (Espressif dropped the classic ESP32's DACs). So the click cannot come off a bare GPIO. Generate it digitally and clock it out over **I2S to a small DAC** (PCM5102 or MAX98357A) → 3.5 mm line out. Bonus: I2S is sample-clocked, so the click edge is sample-accurate.

**Routing — into the kit, not around it.** Feed the click into the **TD-02 `MIX IN`** jack. The module mixes it with its own drum voices and sends both to its output, so the click rides the *existing* wired monitoring chain (TD-02 → Tank-G → headphones). Because click and drums share the identical output path, any monitoring latency (including the Tank-G's digital delay) is **common-mode and cancels** in the drum-vs-click relationship.

**Wired, never Bluetooth.** The click must reach the ears over a **wired** path. Bluetooth audio jitter is variable and **uncalibratable**, which poisons the one thing being measured. Backing tracks may stay on Bluetooth (jitter irrelevant there); the click may not.

**Grading reads MIDI, not audio.** Grading compares MIDI timestamps against the click *schedule*, not the *heard* click — so monitoring latency affects what you hear, not what's scored. Only a single **calibration offset** (audio-out buffer + MIDI-in latency) needs measuring and subtracting. It is **constant for a given setup but not across setups** — audio device, buffer size, and OS state change it — so it is recorded **per session** as `calibration_offset_ms` (brain spec §3/§6), not baked in as one global constant. On the fixed box it will rarely change; on the laptop it changes often, and an *unrecorded* change would masquerade as a rush/drag trend in Layer D — the one confound most able to corrupt the "defensible lane." The drummer plays against the *heard* click, so this offset is embedded in every hit at capture time and is unreconstructable later: by the §1 assignment test, it is capture-time information.

**Metronome/grade separation (brain spec §5).** Sounding the click writes nothing. A **grade-span declaration** snapshots the running click's BPM/subdivision/phase into a grid segment. Warmup-to-a-click is grid-empty and ungraded.

**TD-02 specifics (verified from the module manual):** the TD-02's internal metronome is **audio-only — not transmitted over MIDI** — and the module has **no external MIDI-clock sync**. So you cannot slave the Roland's metronome to the device; the device owns the click entirely. **Turn the Roland's internal metronome OFF** so two independent clocks don't phase against each other.

**PCM5102 bring-up (implemented in firmware; ⚠ VERIFY at wiring, 2026-07-12).** The I2S adapter deliberately emits **no MCLK**, so the PCM5102A must run in PLL mode: **SCK tied to GND** (floating SCK = silent DAC — trap #1); **XSMT high** (un-mute — trap #2); FMT = I2S, DEMP off, FLT normal (breakout solder-jumper names vary — ⚠ VERIFY against the actual board). 16-bit stereo @ 48 kHz is in-range; the 4×64-frame DMA (~5.3 ms) is constant latency and lands in `calibration_offset_ms`. **`click_gain`** (`/config.txt`, 0–100 %, default 50) scales the pre-rendered voices at init: PCM5102A full-scale is ~2.1 Vrms — hotter than the MIX IN wants — and the level that sits right in the TD-02's mix is a *setup* property of this hardware chain, so it is a **firmware-local knob** (never brain/kit-profile data; replacing the DAC re-tunes it here and nowhere else).

## 4A. Laptop-phase capture — the *first* implementation of the capture half

The one-clock rule (§3) and the click (§4) are correctness requirements of the **capture half**, not of the box specifically. In the laptop phase there is no box — but the capture half still exists: it is **the brain-host process wearing its capture hat**, and *it*, not Reaper, must own the click and the clock. This section assigns an ownership that otherwise falls into the gap between the two documents (the brain spec disowns hardware; this doc's §0 disowns the laptop phase).

- **The host process is the tempo authority.** It generates the grading click itself (audio callback) and records `downbeat_t` at click start. **Reaper must not sound the grading click** — if it did, Reaper's downbeat phase would be unknowable to the `mido` capture and the grid would be *estimated*, not exact-by-construction (§4), which defeats the entire reason the tempo authority lives on the capture side. (A DAW may still run in parallel purely for timestamp ground-truth — brain-spec Phase 1 validation — just never as the click source.)
- **One clock (§3 Rule 1, laptop form).** MIDI arrival is stamped in the `mido` input callback, and the click is scheduled, **both against the same monotonic host clock**. Arrival-edge stamping (§3 Rule 2) means: stamp in the input callback, before any file write.
- **Per-session calibration (§4).** The host measures/loads the audio-out + MIDI-in offset for the *current* device/buffer configuration and writes it as `calibration_offset_ms` into session meta.
- **Append-only writer (§6).** The host writes the same append-only typed-line log the box will (brain spec §3), so `FileSource` replay is byte-identical whether the producer was laptop or box.

**Binding:** this is capture implementation **#1**; the box (§9, Phase 2) is implementation **#2**, emitting the *identical* session log. Both obey §3 and §4; only the hardware differs. This is what makes "the brain is built and proven on the laptop, unchanged when the box arrives" (§9) actually true rather than hopeful.

## 5. Control-input / gesture surface

The device needs a control channel for the declarations and boundaries the brain relies on. In the laptop phase this lives in the host; in the standalone box it migrates to firmware (**DECISION 9c**, below).

**Gesture grammar (over the event stream):** session start/stop, bookmark, grade-span start/end, enroll-start/stop. Each is a played pattern chosen to be **un-playable by accident** (e.g. kick+crash×2 to start, per Jamcorder's black-keys-twice precedent; kick=36, crash=49 in the TD-02 map). Implemented as kick+crash chord *repetitions*, timed out after a gap so a longer run isn't mistaken for a shorter one: ×2 = bookmark, ×3 = grade-span toggle, ×4(+) = enroll-span toggle. This is **distinct** from the metronome/tempo control (a knob/app setting that sounds the click and sets BPM/subdivision but writes nothing).

**Session boundaries:** gesture start/stop + an **idle-timeout failsafe** for session end.

**DECISION 9c — where gesture detection lives: RESOLVED for the firmware side.** Gesture detection is a pure state machine over the event stream living in firmware (`edrum_core`, hardware-free, natively tested) — not the host/brain. It was trivial enough in practice to implement directly against the box's own event stream rather than staging it in Python first.

**The control-message vocabulary is source-agnostic by construction (architecture review, 2026-07-11).** Console commands, gestures, and any future controller (desktop/mobile app, hardware button, BLE, network API) all construct the same portable `ControlMsg` (bookmark / grid start-end-toggle / enroll start-end-toggle / end session) and hand it to a single dispatcher, which is the only thing that ever calls into the session FSM. The FSM itself never sees a `ControlMsg` and never knows a controller exists — adding a new controller means teaching it to construct this vocabulary, never touching FSM or dispatch logic. A diagnostic/test-harness command (the synthetic burst load generator, Experiment 3) is deliberately excluded from this vocabulary — it's a firmware self-test concern, not a session declaration any real controller should need to know about.

**Enrollment labeling is optional, and the distinction is honest, not synthesized.** Starting an enrollment span is zero-friction — architecturally the same class as bookmark and grade-span start: no name is required to begin one. If a controller supplies a label (e.g. the CLI's `enroll shuffle`), it is recorded as provenance on the `enroll_start` declaration. If no label is supplied (a toggle gesture, a hardware button, a quick app action — anything with no label slot to offer), the declaration's `profile_ref` is `null` — the capture log honestly represents "no provenance," never a synthesized placeholder like "Enrollment 1". That kind of default naming, along with any later renaming, is entirely the brain/UI's responsibility (brain spec §3/§6): capture-time `profile_ref` is a provenance *hint*, not a welded identity commitment, the same "reassignable, not a welded fact" pattern already used for `kit_profile_id` (§7).

## 6. Storage & session boundaries

**Medium.** SD card (SPI module, or an onboard slot on CAM-style boards). Drum MIDI is **kilobytes** — 8–16 GB is years; don't over-buy. (Jamcorder gets 25,000 hours from 16 GB.)

**Session dating.** Layer D (longitudinal) needs sessions **ordered and dated** — an RTC, or host-provided time at session start. Without it, "session over session" has no axis.

**Borrow from Jamcorder's boundary design** (proven, free to copy): one file per day; keep a session together across midnight; auto-pause after ~3 s silence and resume with no lost events; cache control state (CC/pitch/etc.) while idle and write it on the first note.

**On-disk format = the append-only log (brain spec §3).** The writer only ever **appends** typed lines (`meta`, `event`, `ctrl`, declarations, `session_end`); it never rewrites line 1. This is what makes the ring-buffer→SD path (§2) crash-survivable: on power loss — a certainty for an unplugged standalone box — the recovery rule is simply *truncate to the last complete line* and the session is valid up to that point. Grid segments, bookmarks, and enrollment spans are written **as they are declared, mid-session** (they are declaration lines), never buffered in RAM to be flushed into a header at close — a design that would lose every declaration on the exact crash the ring buffer exists to survive. The cached control state above is written as `ctrl` lines. And the auto-pause/resume is an **annotation on the timeline** — the session clock `t` keeps counting through the pause (brain spec §3), so IOI and grid math span pauses correctly; pause never rebases the clock.

## 7. Kit-agnostic emission

The device emits **raw-as-received** `(t, note, velocity)` for hits **and raw non-note channel messages** (`ctrl` occurrences — hi-hat pedal-position CC, cymbal-choke aftertouch, positional-sensing CCs; brain spec §3) + a **reassignable `kit_profile_id`** pointer, and nothing kit-specific beyond that tag. The `ctrl` messages ride through untouched for the same reason the timestamp does: they are stream information *destroyed if not captured* (§1 assignment test), so the dumb device commits them raw and the brain decides later what — if anything — to derive from them (HH openness, chokes, positional grading). The device does **not** interpret them. Normalization is brain-side (brain spec §4.2), so the **capture firmware is identical across all kits** — a new kit is a brain-side profile addition, never a firmware fork. The `kit_profile_id` is a *hint the brain can re-point*, not a welded fact, because it's the one interpretive bit the device asserts and it can be mislabeled.

Note "raw" is **post-kit-firmware**: the module already applied its zone-mapping and velocity curve before the byte left. That's un-removable and it's why cross-kit velocity comparability is a brain-side calibration, not a device guarantee.

## 8. Hardware decision log

*The expensive, hard-to-reconstruct part. Confidence flagged; **⚠ VERIFY** = confirm against the actual board/schematic before committing.*

### 8.1 USB topology — the device must be a USB *host*
The TD-02 is **USB-MIDI-out only** (no 5-pin DIN to tap). So the capture device must **host** a class-compliant USB-MIDI device — not be a USB device itself. USB-*device* mode (being read by a PC) is trivial and is **not** what this needs; USB-*host* mode (reading the Roland) is the real requirement. The capstone manuscript's "interrupt-driven MIDI *UART* capture" does **not** apply — there's no DIN/UART line; it's USB host.

Two ways to host:
- **Native (S3 USB-OTG).** Library `ESP32_Host_MIDI` (needs arduino-esp32 ≥ 3.0, USB Mode = Host) makes it ~a few lines. Background-buffered, clean timestamps by default.
- **External (MAX3421E over SPI).** `USB Host Shield 2.0` + `USBH_MIDI`. Poll-based (`Usb.Task()`), needs a 12 MHz-crystal breakout, ~6 pins, external VBUS, and 5 V-tolerant wiring/level-shifting. **On the S3 it is capability-redundant** (the S3 already hosts) — you'd choose it only to (a) free the native USB for normal flashing and (b) decouple USB from the MCU. See 8.5.

### 8.2 VBUS — the recurring trap (applies to *every* board)
USB enumeration is gated on the **device seeing ~5 V on VBUS**. The TD-02 is self-powered (own adapter), so you only need to **present** ~5 V for the handshake, not power the kit.
- **Nano ESP32 (ABX00083):** VBUS is **disabled when powered via VIN** (deliberate backfeed protection — confirmed from official docs). OTG adapter + VIN power ⇒ **0 V VBUS ⇒ kit stays electrically dead.**
- **DevKitC-1:** the native USB port does **not** supply VBUS to a downstream device by default either (Espressif forum reports confirm). Same failure.
- **The fix (same on both):** inject 5 V into the host connection's VBUS externally — a **powered / Y-OTG adapter** with a second port for a 5 V feed (charger, power bank, spare USB). D+/D− still pass through. **⚠ VERIFY:** a clone DevKitC (YD-ESP32-S3) has a rear **solder jumper** bridging a diode to route board-5 V to VBUS; this was **not** confirmed on the official Espressif board — don't rely on it without checking your unit.

### 8.3 Flashing while hosting — the single-USB contention
The S3 has **one** USB peripheral; hosting the module consumes it, so it can't also be the flash/serial port.
- **Nano (one USB-C):** either juggle (unplug module → flash → replug, with a BOOT+RESET dance to enter the bootloader) **or** build a **CP2102/CH340 dongle + 2-cap auto-reset circuit** (RTS→EN, DTR→GPIO0 through 100 nF caps) to flash over UART while the module stays hosted. ~1 hour of fiddling; occasionally needs the transistor-pair variant.
- **DevKitC-1 (two USB-C):** a **separate CP210x UART-bridge port** with auto-reset built in handles flash + serial while the native port hosts the module. **No juggle, no dongle, no circuit.** This is the ~₱400 win, and it's the *only* clear advantage of the DevKitC over the Nano.
- **Wireless alternatives (Nano, no dongle):** stream debug over **WiFi** (TCP/telnet) and flash via **OTA** after one cable flash — then the module can stay hosted permanently with no cable. Cost: OTA setup + occasional recovery cable.

### 8.4 Final-assembly wiring & pin access
- **DevKitC-1:** breaks out **GPIO19/20** (native USB D−/D+) to the headers, so you can solder your own **USB-A panel receptacle** (D− to 19, D+ to 20, GND common, VBUS to your 5 V rail) for a clean enclosure. **⚠ VERIFY:** confirm the onboard USB-C port doesn't fight the header pins when both are wired (cut a trace / use only one) — check continuity before committing.
- **Nano ESP32:** does **not** break out the USB D+/D− pins (only 24 GPIOs; USB/LED/flash pins aren't on the headers). So the *only* path to the kit in a final build is a **USB-C OTG adapter permanently attached** to the USB-C connector — a functional but franken dangling-adapter, not a clean panel jack.
- **Power in final assembly:** since the native USB points at the module, the board needs its **own 5 V feed** (Nano's VBUS-block is moot here because you wire your 5 V rail straight to the receptacle's VBUS pin, not through the board).

### 8.5 Board choice — the tradeoff
- **Nano ESP32 (owned, ₱0 + no shipping):** smallest final footprint, but the flash-juggle (or a self-built auto-reset circuit), external VBUS injection, and **no D+/D− breakout** (permanent OTG dongle in the final box).
- **ESP32-S3-DevKitC-1 (~₱400 + shipping):** two-port flashing (the real win), D+/D− on headers for a clean receptacle; still needs external VBUS injection; bigger.
- **MAX3421E (external host):** frees native USB (normal flashing, no juggle), **decouples USB from the MCU** (capture subsystem ports to *any* SPI MCU), and is the most educational (register-level USB host + cross-chip SPI comms — closest to the stated curiosity). Costs: bigger box, poll-based (ring-buffer discipline mandatory), **must timestamp on INT** (§3), extra chip/crystal/level-shift. Capability-redundant on the S3.
- **CAM-style N16R8 board** (e.g. "…ESP32-S3 N16R8 CAM"): tempting for its **onboard microSD slot** + 8 MB PSRAM + dual USB-C. **⚠ VERIFY (make-or-break):** confirm the SD slot's pins **don't collide with GPIO19/20** (the native USB D+/D−) and that it's **SDMMC** (keyestudio variant: CLK 39 / CMD 38 / D0 40 — no USB collision, but SDMMC not SPI, and camera-bus-shared). **⚠ VERIFY:** that the second USB-C is a real UART bridge (often CH340), not power-only. Documentation risk is high on no-name units — if you can't get its pinout, the risk outweighs the SD-slot convenience.

### 8.6 Monitor path (his rig)
Kit → **Tank-G** (M-VAVE guitar multi-FX, used as clean pass-through + monitor) → headphones; iPhone backing tracks over the Tank-G's **Bluetooth**. The click enters at **TD-02 MIX IN** and rides the existing wired chain to the Tank-G, so it shares the drums' output path (latency cancels, §4) and stays off Bluetooth.

## 9. Deployment progression

- **Phase 1 — laptop.** "Capture device" = kit → Reaper/`mido` on a computer. No box. The brain is built and proven here (brain spec Phases 0–5).
- **Phase 2 — the box.** ESP32-S3 emits the *same* session files; the brain reads files instead of a live port, unchanged. This is the contained firmware spike (USB host, VBUS, ring buffer, INT-timestamp, click DAC, SD).
- **Possible v1 shortcut:** kit → phone via USB adapter, phone app does capture + review — may need **no custom hardware at all**. The box only earns its place for the no-PC/no-phone standalone unit or the firmware learning.

## 12. Enrollment control-surface architecture review (2026-07-11)

A senior-architecture review of the enrollment control path, resolved and implemented:

- **Enrollment is zero-friction to start, like bookmark/grade-span** — but its cross-session identity (the join key Layer C uses to fold repeated demonstrations of the same groove into one profile, brain spec §6) is a genuinely different concern from "start capturing now," and conflating the two is what forced a name upfront originally. Splitting them — instant start, optional label — is what §5 now specifies.
- **`ControlMsg` moved into the portable core** (`edrum_core/control_msg.h`), not the composition root, so it's the shared vocabulary every controller (console, gesture, future desktop/mobile/BLE/network) constructs against, and it's native-testable independent of any hardware.
- **A single `ControlDispatcher`** (`edrum_core/control_dispatch.h/.cpp`) is the only thing that translates a `ControlMsg` into `SessionController` calls, resolving the running-click snapshot via a new narrow port (`hal::IClickSnapshot`) along the way. The session FSM's own API is deliberately **not** `ControlMsg`-shaped — it kept its existing typed methods (`bookmark(t)`, `grid_start(t,bpm,subdiv,downbeat)`, ...), which is what makes it trivial to drive from native tests and keeps a flat "one struct for every op" shape out of the FSM's own interface. This closed a real gap: control-routing logic (including the click-snapshot-refusal path) was previously only reachable on hardware (`capture_task.cpp`'s `handle_control`); it's now natively covered (`test/native/test_control_dispatch`).
- **The synthetic burst load generator (Experiment 3) was removed from `ControlMsg`** and given its own composition-root-local message (`DiagMsg`) — it's a firmware self-test concern, not a session declaration, and didn't belong in the vocabulary every future controller has to learn.
- **Gesture grammar extended:** kick+crash ×4(+) → enroll-span toggle, alongside the existing ×2 → bookmark, ×3 → grade-span toggle (§5). A toggle-triggered enrollment is always anonymous — a gesture has no label slot to offer by construction; a controller that has one (the CLI, a future app) uses the labeled start instead.
- **Schema bump to v2** (`kSchemaVersion` / `SCHEMA_VERSION`): `enroll_start.profile_ref` became nullable. This is a *meaning* change to an existing field's value domain, not an additive one (brain spec §3's schema-evolution rule), so it earns the major bump rather than riding in for free — a v1-only reader that assumed a non-empty string would mishandle `null` silently otherwise.

Full rationale, including why groove-identity resolution belongs brain-side as a revisable mapping over `(session_id, start_t)` rather than in the immutable log, lives in brain spec §3/§6.

## 13. Device↔brain link — the session-archive sync (2026-07-12, implemented)

The first communication path between the box and the brain, designed as three deliberately separate layers so future transports plug in without touching the capture core or the brain.

**13.1 The logical contract: archive replication, byte-exact.** The seam is still the session file (§0) — so the link's only job is to **move the canonical bytes, unchanged**. The device is the writer-of-record; the brain replicates its archive. The acid test of transport-agnosticism: *pulling the SD card and copying files with a card reader is the degenerate, zero-code implementation of this contract* — every real transport must produce results indistinguishable from sneakernet (same names, same bytes, landing in `sessions/`). Anything that re-serializes or wraps records would create a second dialect of the corpus contract and a second thing to keep conformant. Corollary (one-clock, §3): the device **never streams raw MIDI to the brain** — that would put two clocks on one performance and two writers-of-record on one session.

Contract semantics (v1): **closed sessions only** (the open file is excluded from `LIST`; a live tail is a reserved v2 extension and must be a byte-level tee of the same `LogWriter` output — brain spec Phase 7's "another thin consumer of the identical stream"); **read-only** (no delete/rename — retention is a user act, not protocol); **idempotent + resumable** (skip by size+checksum; resume by verified prefix; the brain never overwrites an existing local file — same bytes = skip, different bytes = loud conflict); **host time rides along** (the handshake carries host wall time = automatic `settime`, healing ADR-5 dating on every sync); **versioned like the schema** (protocol + `schema_version` exchanged at HELLO; newer major refused explicitly, mirroring brain spec §3's reader policy).

**13.2 The transport abstraction.** `hal::IByteLink` — a plain byte pipe (non-blocking `read`, `write`), the least common denominator every candidate transport offers (UART, USB-CDC, TCP, BLE characteristic pair). Framing does **not** live in adapters: COBS + CRC-16/CCITT-FALSE sit once in the portable core (`edrum_core/frame.*`, delimiter-resynchronizable, natively tested with vectors shared byte-for-byte with the brain's mirror), and the protocol engine (`edrum_core/sync_service.*`) is a pure request→response state machine over a second narrow port, `hal::ISessionArchive` (list/size/read — deliberately separate from `IStorage`, whose single-open-file append-only model is load-bearing and must not widen into a filesystem API). Per-request work bounds (FETCH ≤ 512 B, CRC ≤ 4 KiB chunk-chained) cap how long one storage-task iteration holds the SD. The frame header carries a channel byte; channel 1 is reserved for a future remote control plane, which already has its vocabulary (`ControlMsg`, §5/§12) — v1 makes no other provision.

**Concurrency:** the sync service runs *inside the storage task loop* — ring drain unconditionally first (capture always wins), then at most one bounded link chunk. Single SD owner by construction; a transfer physically cannot smear a timestamp (wrong side of the ring, wrong core) or starve the log.

**Fail-soft (storage policy, resolved 2026-07-12):** if the card is missing/full/corrupt, the `LogWriter` latches `failed()` — records **discard gracefully and are counted** (`storage_discarded`), never hammering the dead card, and the next session retries; capture, gestures, and the click keep running untouched (the box degrades to a fully functional standalone metronome + processor). A sync attempted in this state gets an explicit `ERR StorageFailed` **on the wire** — never a hang.

**13.3 Implementation #1: serial, on the console line (modal).** The UART bridge port already validated by ADR-4 — zero new hardware, zero radio stack; deliberately the least interesting transport because the *interfaces* are the deliverable. Arbitration is modal (resolved 2026-07-12): the console `sync` command suspends interactive console I/O and hands the line to the framed protocol; `BYE` or a 10 s idle timeout hands it back. During framed mode all task console prints are suppressed (text inside a frame would corrupt it); declarations still apply, silently. The brain side (`edrum/io/devlink.py` + `edrum sync --port COMx`, `[device]` extra) automates the handoff by typing `sync` itself, verifies each landed file (chunk CRC → whole-file CRC → parse), and the definitive gate stays what it always was: `edrum replay` byte-identity. `engine/` and the `Source` seam are untouched — "the box's files arrive through `FileSource` unchanged" stays literally true.
